Don’t Look Away Now —

The Crisis of Homelessness in the United States

By Caroline Abel, Charles Onesti, and Emma Ton

Despite having the world's largest economy with a 2020 GDP of 23 trillion dollars, there are more than 580,000 people experiencing homelessness in the U.S. on any given day.

Homelessness by the Numbers

Select one or all states and adjust the slider to see how the number of people living in different homelessness situations has changed from 2007 to 2020

This chart displays the number of homeless peole in various categories using representative symbols of 5000. Each dot represents 5000 people.

People in a Emergency Shelter are those given temporary shelter when in economic or domestic distress. They are provided short term stability and are not forced to sign a lease or living agreement.

In Transitional Housing, people are given temporary shelter for 6 to 24 months and are often supported in finding employment.

Unsheltered people are those who reside in areas not meant for habitation such as cars, abandoned buildings, or on the street.

Let's Take a Closer Look at Patterns in Homelessness

Gender and Race Breakdown of the U.S. Homeless Population in 2021

Hover over the legends below to highlight the corresponding sectors of the pie charts. See each sector for details on the demographics.

The gender pie chart shows the share of homeless in the U.S. in 2021 who identify as Male, Female, or Other Genders (including Transgender or Gender Non Conforming). The vast majority of homeless identify as Male (56%) followed by Female (43.4%).

The race pie chart shows the breakdown by race of homeless in 2021. The majority of homeless people identify as either White or Black. The third largest number of homeless people identify as Hispanic or Latino.

Between 2007 and 2020, the number of people experiencing homelessness in the U.S. peaked in California, followed by Texas, Florida, and New York.

Policies and historical events had the potential to change homelessness

Recent Policies and Events and their Effect on Overall Homelessness

Hover the event flags to learn more about historical policies and events. Click the axis button to zoom in and see the yearly differences in greater relative magnitude. Each bar will also give you specifics on the data point from that year.

The bars on the timeline above show the amount of total homelessness in that year.

Overall homelessness numbers have not changed substantially over the years despite new policies and events. If interested in seeing yearly changes close up, click the zoom button. Notice how homelessness fell slightly from 2012 to 2017 during greater economic growth in the US and has recently been rising perhaps from the public health crises and slowed economic growth

Despite the policies in place, homelessness is a persistent problem. It takes a larger movement combining the efforts from both the government and the public. Our goal is to raise awareness about the homelessness problem.

What Can You Do?

Thousands of people across the country are homeless with limited help in sight.

Here are some things you can do to help:

Volunteer at a soup kitchen, homeless shelter, or charities involved in helping the homeless.

Fundraise for charities helping the homeless

Donate clothes and food.

Educate yourself and others about the homeless population and the homelessness issue.

Contact your representative to advocate for policies that help the homeless!
